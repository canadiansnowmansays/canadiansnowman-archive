The Million-Dollar Senate Grift

When Corruption Becomes Law

How Republican Senators Tried to Write Themselves a Check for Being Lawfully Investigated

November 23, 2025

Senate Majority Leader John Thune (R-SD) inserted the self-enrichment provision into a 394-page spending bill in December 2024. While Thune’s own phone records were not obtained in the investigation, his provision allows the nine senators whose records were seized to sue for retroactive damages—creating a two-tier justice system that applies only to members of Congress.

Sitting here in Toronto, watching American politics has become an exercise in recalibrating what counts as shocking. But even by 2025 standards, what Senate Majority Leader John Thune pulled off in the dying hours of the government shutdown negotiations represents a new low. He didn’t just cross ethical lines - he bulldozed through them with a bulldozer while flipping off the Constitution.

Buried on page 287 of a 394-page appropriations bill, inserted without consulting Democrats or informing the public, was a provision allowing U.S. senators to sue the federal government for up to one million dollars if their phone records were subpoenaed without notification - even when those subpoenas were completely lawful and judicially authorized. Not House members. Not ordinary citizens facing the same investigative techniques every day. Not even other federal officials. Just senators. Just the most powerful legislators in the country, creating a special carve-out for themselves.

This is the legislative equivalent of a bank robber rewriting the criminal code mid-heist to make stealing legal, then suing the police for having the audacity to investigate. And the most infuriating part? This scandal has been grossly underreported and under-condemned, lost in the noise of our 24/7 outrage cycle where healthcare subsidies, tariffs, and celebrity scandals dominate the headlines. So let me break down exactly what happened here, why it matters, and why every American should be furious.

The Setup: How We Got Here

Here’s the backstory. During his investigation into the January 6th coup attempt, Special Counsel Jack Smith sought phone records of several members of Congress who may have participated in or had knowledge of the conspiracy. In May 2023, Judge James Boasberg approved the subpoenas, explicitly finding Smith’s approach entirely lawful, proper, and consistent with established DOJ policy.

Judge Boasberg also approved a non-disclosure order preventing carriers from tipping off the targets. Why? Because there were reasonable grounds to believe disclosure would lead to evidence destruction, witness intimidation, and serious jeopardy to the investigation. This is standard procedure in complex investigations, particularly those involving potential coordination among multiple subjects.

What did these subpoenas actually seek? Metadata only - phone numbers, call duration, nothing more. Not conversation content. Not privileged communications. Just the digital equivalent of the outside of an envelope. The nine senators whose records were obtained: Lindsay Graham, Bill Hagerty, Josh Hawley, Dan Sullivan, Tommy Tuberville, Ron Johnson, Cynthia Lummis, Marsha Blackburn, and Rep. Mike Kelly. Ted Cruz and Rick Scott were also targets but their records apparently weren’t provided.

These nine Republican senators and one representative had their phone records obtained by Special Counsel Jack Smith during the January 6 investigation. Senate Majority Leader John Thune inserted a provision in the December 2024 continuing resolution allowing them to sue for up to $1 million each in retroactive damages. The House voted 426-0 to repeal the provision, but Senator Lindsey Graham is personally blocking the Senate vote—while announcing plans to sue for ‘tens of millions’ himself.

When the non-disclosure order expired and they were informed, these senators erupted in performative outrage. Never mind that every single step was judicially authorized and followed DOJ protocols. They’d been investigated, and that was intolerable.

Enter John Thune and the government shutdown crisis. With the clock ticking, Thune saw his opening. Without informing Democrats, he slipped his provision into the appropriations bill: senators could now sue for up to 500,000 dollars for records illegally obtained and another 500,000 dollars for failure to notify. Notification could only be delayed if the individual was a criminal investigation subject. Oh, and make it retroactive to 2022, perfectly timed to cover Smith’s investigation.

Why This Is Blatantly Unconstitutional

Creating a Privileged Class

The Equal Protection Clause isn’t complicated: the law must treat similarly situated people similarly. This provision obliterates that principle by creating an explicit two-tier justice system. Senators get million-dollar paydays. House members get nothing. State legislators get nothing. Ordinary citizens who have their phone records subpoenaed in criminal investigations - which happens thousands of times per year - get nothing.

Even House Judiciary Chair Jim Jordan - hardly a voice of moderation and someone who thinks Boasberg’s order was unjustified - called this out. We should pass laws for Americans, he said, not for any special category. When Jim Jordan is the voice of constitutional reason, you know you’ve gone off the rails.

Destroying Sovereign Immunity

Sovereign immunity holds that government can’t be sued without its consent. This doctrine, embedded in American law since the founding, protects the public treasury, prevents judicial second-guessing of every executive decision, and ensures elected officials - not judges responding to lawsuits - make policy.

Congress can waive sovereign immunity, but historically only in carefully limited circumstances with compelling public policy justifications. This waiver does the opposite: it enriches powerful senators who disliked being lawfully investigated. It applies only to a tiny elite. And it sets a catastrophic precedent.

Consider the floodgates. If senators can sue for damages when subjected to lawful investigative techniques, why can’t House members? Federal judges whose finances are reviewed during ethics probes? Cabinet secretaries whose emails are subpoenaed? Once you establish that Congress can waive sovereign immunity whenever its members feel aggrieved by lawful government action, there’s no logical stopping point.

Retroactive Revenge Legislation

Making this retroactive to 2022 is the poison cherry on top. The principle that laws operate prospectively, not retrospectively, runs deep in Anglo-American legal tradition. The Constitution prohibits ex post facto criminal laws for good reason.

But the retroactivity here is uniquely egregious: it penalizes conduct that was judicially authorized when it occurred. Judge Boasberg specifically found Smith’s methods lawful. The carriers followed valid court orders. Nobody broke any rules. Yet now Congress wants to create liability and award damages for following the law. This isn’t reform - it’s legislative revenge dressed up as policy.

The Corruption Pattern

Senate Ethics Committee Rule 37 explicitly prohibits members from advancing legislation that produces direct personal financial gain. This isn’t obscure fine print buried in some dusty manual - it’s a bedrock anti-corruption principle designed to prevent exactly this kind of self-dealing. That Thune felt comfortable inserting this provision despite the obvious violation tells you everything about how far institutional guardrails have collapsed. The guardrails aren’t just weakened; they’ve been demolished and hauled away for scrap.

To their minimal credit, most targeted senators recognized the toxic optics and announced they wouldn’t pursue compensation. Blackburn and Sullivan even said they’d vote for repeal. But Lindsay Graham and Tommy Tuberville? They apparently possess so little capacity for shame that they made no such commitments. Graham has gone further: when the House passed a unanimous repeal this week, Graham personally blocked it in the Senate. He then announced plans to sue not just for the 500,000 dollars specified in the provision, but for tens of millions of dollars. He’s also planning to sue Verizon for cooperating with lawful court orders.

Think about that sequence. Graham, who has transformed himself from Trump critic to MAGA sycophant with such thorough completeness that it would be impressive if it weren’t so pathetic, is now blocking legislation that passed the House 426-0 because he wants his multi-million dollar payday. This isn’t just shameless - it’s corruption so brazen it mocks the very concept of public service.

This scandal doesn’t exist in isolation. It’s part of a systematic pattern we’ve documented extensively: members trading stocks based on confidential briefings about impending crises, family members landing lucrative contracts and board positions they’re spectacularly unqualified for, the revolving door between Congress and lobbying firms spinning at warp speed. Each instance gets rationalized or drowned out by the next outrage. But collectively, they reveal a political class that has divorced itself from any meaningful conception of public service. The question isn’t whether they’ll abuse power - it’s only which abuse we’ll catch them at next.

When senators can literally write themselves million-dollar checks from the public treasury for the crime of being lawfully investigated for their role in an attempted coup, we’ve crossed a threshold. This is corruption so brazen it doesn’t bother with the usual pretense of deniability. It’s corruption that looks you dead in the eye and dares you to do something about it.

What Happens Next - And What Just Happened

The House voted 426 to 0 - read that again, unanimous - to repeal this abomination on November 19. Not a single representative, Republican or Democrat, was willing to defend this naked corruption on the record. That should tell you everything about how indefensible this provision is.

But here’s where it gets even more outrageous. This week, when the repeal bill reached the Senate, Democratic Senator Martin Heinrich tried to pass it by unanimous consent. One senator blocked it: Lindsey Graham. The same Lindsey Graham who’s one of the primary beneficiaries. The same Graham who then went on Fox News and announced he plans to sue for tens of millions of dollars - far exceeding the 500,000 dollars specified in the provision.

Let that sink in. Graham is blocking the repeal of a law that will personally enrich him by tens of millions of dollars. He’s also planning to sue Verizon for cooperating with a lawful, judicially-authorized investigation. On the Senate floor, he had the audacity to ask, What did I do wrong? What did I do to allow the government to seize my personal phone?

Here’s what you did wrong, Senator: you participated in or enabled an attempted coup, and when investigators used standard, lawful techniques to investigate, you’re now trying to profit from it. And you’re blocking legislation that passed the House unanimously because you want your payday.

Thune tried to offer a compromise: redirect any damages to the Treasury instead of senators’ pockets. Heinrich rightfully rejected this fig leaf. The underlying constitutional violations remain: the creation of a privileged class, the destruction of sovereign immunity, the retroactive revenge legislation. Moving money from Column A to Column B doesn’t fix the fundamental corruption.

If the repeal somehow survives Graham’s obstruction and reaches Trump’s desk, he faces an interesting choice. He has every political incentive to sign it - punishing Jack Smith’s investigation would appeal to his vindictive streak. But even Trump’s lawyers might recognize the dangerous precedent. If Congress can waive sovereign immunity against prosecutors who followed the law, what happens when a Democratic Congress applies the same principle to investigations of Trump? The sword cuts both ways.

Why This Matters

From here in Canada, I’ve spent years documenting American political corruption - Trump’s systematic grift, the accommodation of fascist rhetoric, the wholesale abandonment of constitutional principles. But this Senate provision represents something particularly corrosive: the open codification of a two-tier system where the powerful write their own rules and immunize themselves from consequences.

The social contract underlying democratic governance rests on elected officials serving the public interest and submitting to accountability. When that premise becomes untenable, when citizens see that those who govern view public office as a vehicle for self-enrichment, democratic legitimacy cracks. Why respect laws passed by legislators who exempt themselves? This breeds the corrosive cynicism that makes democracies vulnerable to demagogues promising to burn it all down.

The Senate’s self-enrichment scheme is a symptom revealing the underlying disease. It’s relatively small in dollar terms but enormous in what it signals: senators feel comfortable engaging in naked self-dealing openly, in legislation requiring their own votes, because institutional norms have deteriorated to the point where consequences don’t exist.

The Bottom Line

This story deserves sustained outrage. The fact that it’s being buried in the news cycle - overshadowed by whatever fresh hell Trump tweeted this morning or which celebrity said something stupid - is itself a scandal. We’ve become so numb to corruption that even a senator blocking unanimous legislation to preserve his multi-million dollar payday barely registers.

We need to be crystal clear about what’s happening: a handful of Republican senators who participated in or enabled a coup attempt are now trying to profit from being investigated for it. The House voted 426-0 to stop this. Lindsey Graham single-handedly blocked that repeal because he personally stands to gain tens of millions of dollars. They’re using their legislative power to retroactively penalize prosecutors who followed the law, create a privileged class exempt from normal accountability, and raid the public treasury for personal enrichment.

This isn’t politics as usual. This isn’t partisan gridlock or good-faith policy disagreement. This is corruption, pure and simple. It’s the kind of self-dealing you’d expect from a kleptocracy. And if we accept it, if we let it fade into the background noise, we’re conceding that American democracy has become a facade behind which a political class enriches itself while ordinary citizens bear the consequences.

The Senate may have sunk to new lows, but it doesn’t have to stay there. Whether it rises depends on whether enough senators - and enough citizens - decide that some principles are worth defending even when politically inconvenient. Without accountability, every other political debate becomes meaningless.

The world’s supposedly greatest deliberative body has become a vehicle for shameless self-enrichment. We cannot afford to look away.
