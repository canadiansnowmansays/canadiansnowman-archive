Georgia RICO

Part 15: The Indictment (2021-2024)

January 27, 2026

Georgia Secretary of State Brad Raffensperger

On January 2, 2021, Donald Trump placed a phone call to Georgia Secretary of State Brad Raffensperger. What Trump said on that call—recorded and later released publicly—was extraordinary:

“I just want to find 11,780 votes, which is one more than we have... Fellas, I need 11,000 votes. Give me a break.”

For over an hour, Trump pressured Georgia’s top election official to “find” exactly the number of votes Trump needed to flip Georgia from Biden to Trump. He made false claims of fraud. He implied Raffensperger could face criminal liability. He threatened Raffensperger’s lawyer.

It was, legal experts said, a clear-cut case of criminal election interference.

The phone call alone—documented, recorded, undeniable—provided the basis for prosecution under Georgia law.

Two and a half years later, on August 14, 2023, Fulton County District Attorney Fani Willis unveiled a sprawling 98-page indictment charging Trump and 18 others with racketeering and conspiracy to overturn Georgia’s 2020 election results.

The case seemed strong. The evidence was extensive. And unlike federal prosecutions, Georgia’s state case was insulated from Department of Justice policies about prosecuting sitting presidents.

But on November 26, 2025—today—the case was dismissed entirely.

This is the story of how the last criminal prosecution of Donald Trump collapsed—not because of weak evidence, but because of a district attorney’s romantic relationship and the practical impossibility of prosecuting a sitting president in state court.

The RICO Indictment: 19 Defendants, 161 Criminal Acts

On August 14, 2023, a Fulton County grand jury returned an indictment charging Trump and 18 co-defendants with violating Georgia’s Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations (RICO) Act.

The Defendants Included:

  • Donald Trump - Former President

  • Rudy Giuliani - Trump’s personal attorney, former NYC Mayor

  • Mark Meadows - White House Chief of Staff

  • John Eastman - Attorney who authored election-overturning memos

  • Sidney Powell - Attorney who pushed conspiracy theories

  • Kenneth Chesebro - Attorney who organized fake electors

  • Jenna Ellis - Trump campaign attorney

  • Jeffrey Clark - Former DOJ official

  • Plus 11 others including lawyers, political operatives, and activists

The indictment alleged a criminal enterprise designed to overturn Georgia’s election results through 161 overt criminal acts.

The Evidence: A Criminal Conspiracy on Tape

Unlike many criminal cases that rely on circumstantial evidence, Georgia’s case against Trump was built on direct, incontrovertible proof.

1. The Raffensperger Phone Call

The centerpiece was Trump’s January 2, 2021 call to Secretary of State Raffensperger. The call was recorded. Trump’s voice. Trump’s words. Trump’s demands.

Key excerpts:

  • “I just want to find 11,780 votes” (exactly the margin Biden won by)

  • “You know what they did and you’re not reporting it. That’s a criminal offense”

  • “The people of Georgia are angry, the people in the country are angry”

  • When Raffensperger’s lawyer pushed back, Trump said: “Well, I don’t think you’re showing... you’re not showing the kind of... the spirit that you should”

Legal experts called it solicitation of election fraud—a clear violation of Georgia criminal law.

2. The Fake Electors Scheme

On December 14, 2020, while legitimate electors were casting Georgia’s 16 electoral votes for Biden, 16 Georgia Republicans met at the State Capitol and signed false certificates claiming they were Georgia’s “duly elected and qualified” electors.

Biden had won Georgia by 11,779 votes. The result had been certified. The fake electors had no legal authority.

But they signed documents claiming Trump won Georgia and submitted them to Congress and the National Archives. The scheme was designed to create confusion on January 6th and give Vice President Pence a pretext to reject legitimate electoral votes.

3. Harassment of Ruby Freeman

Trump and Giuliani made false accusations against election worker Ruby Freeman and her daughter Wandeshaye “Shaye” Moss, claiming they engaged in fraud during vote counting.

The accusations were completely false. But they led to:

  • Death threats against Freeman

  • Racist attacks

  • Freeman having to leave her home for safety

  • Surveillance by Trump operatives at Freeman’s home

Freeman later won a $148 million defamation judgment against Giuliani in federal court. The jury found Giuliani had destroyed her life with lies.

4. The Coffee County Breach

In January 2021, Trump operatives allegedly breached election equipment in Coffee County, Georgia, copying software and data. This potentially compromised election security and violated computer crime laws.

5. False Testimony to State Legislators

Trump and Giuliani appeared before Georgia legislators on December 3, 2020, making false claims of widespread fraud. Video shows Giuliani presenting a selectively-edited video he claimed showed fraud—the same video featuring Ruby Freeman that he later defamed her over.

The testimony was designed to pressure legislators to overturn Biden’s victory.

The Strength of Georgia’s Case

Unlike the federal prosecutions, Georgia’s case had unique advantages:

State Sovereignty: Federal DOJ policies about not prosecuting sitting presidents don’t apply to state cases. Georgia could theoretically prosecute Trump even as president.

The Recorded Evidence: The Raffensperger call was on tape. A jury could hear Trump’s own voice demanding election fraud.

The Fake Elector Documents: Sixteen people signed false certificates. Those documents exist. The conspiracy was documented in writing.

Cooperative Witnesses: Four co-defendants took plea deals, including three attorneys directly involved:

  • Sidney Powell (pleaded guilty to six misdemeanors)

  • Kenneth Chesebro (pleaded guilty to one felony)

  • Jenna Ellis (pleaded guilty to one felony)

  • Scott Hall (pleaded guilty to five misdemeanors)

These cooperators could testify against Trump and others, describing the conspiracy from inside.

Local Victims: Georgia officials—Raffensperger, Governor Brian Kemp, election workers—could testify about Trump’s pressure and harassment campaigns.

Legal analysts called it a strong, comprehensive case. “This was the one that really worried Trump,” one expert noted, “because he couldn’t make it go away by winning the presidency.”

Or so everyone thought.

Enter Fani Willis: The Prosecutor Who Built the Case

Fulton County District Attorney Fani Willis at the county courthouse in Atlanta on Aug. 8, 2023 (Megan Varner for The Washington Post)

Fani Willis was elected Fulton County District Attorney in 2020. She built her reputation prosecuting complex RICO cases, including a massive Atlanta teacher cheating scandal.

Willis was ambitious, aggressive, and unafraid of powerful defendants. In February 2021, she opened an investigation into Trump’s efforts to overturn Georgia’s election.

She convened a special purpose grand jury with subpoena power. Over 18 months, the grand jury heard from 75 witnesses including:

  • Georgia Secretary of State Brad Raffensperger

  • Governor Brian Kemp

  • Senators and state officials

  • Trump campaign staff

  • The fake electors

  • Election workers

Willis assembled a massive case file: testimony, documents, recordings, video evidence.

On August 14, 2023, at a dramatic late-night press conference, Willis announced the indictment. Standing beside her was Nathan Wade, the special prosecutor she had appointed to lead the case.

The indictment was sweeping. Nineteen defendants. Thirteen counts against Trump personally. Using Georgia’s RICO statute to tie together a conspiracy spanning from Election Day 2020 through January 6, 2021.

Trump’s Mugshot: An Iconic Moment

On August 24, 2023, Trump surrendered at the Fulton County Jail. For just over 20 minutes, he was processed, fingerprinted, and photographed.

The resulting mugshot—Trump glowering at the camera, prisoner number P01135809—became instantly iconic. Trump used it for fundraising, selling merchandise with his mugshot to supporters who saw it as proof of persecution.

All 19 defendants pleaded not guilty. Trump’s lead attorney Steve Sadow called the charges “baseless” and predicted vindication.

For several months, the case proceeded normally. Judge Scott McAfee held hearings. Both sides filed motions. Discovery proceeded.

Then in January 2024, a defense attorney detonated a bomb that would ultimately destroy the entire prosecution.

The Bombshell: Romance Derails Justice

On January 8, 2024, Ashleigh Merchant—attorney for co-defendant Michael Roman—filed a motion seeking Fani Willis’s removal from the case.

The motion’s allegations were explosive:

The Relationship: Willis had engaged in a romantic relationship with Nathan Wade, the special prosecutor she personally hired to lead the Trump prosecution.

The Money: Willis hired Wade despite his lack of experience with major RICO cases. Wade was paid over $650,000 in county funds for his work on the case.

The Benefit: Wade used his earnings to pay for expensive vacations with Willis, including trips to:

  • Napa Valley wine country

  • Aruba

  • Belize

  • Caribbean cruises

The Conflict: Willis therefore personally benefited when her romantic partner billed the county for work on her case. This created an appearance of impropriety and potential conflict of interest.

The allegations were devastating. If true, Willis had:

  1. Hired her boyfriend to prosecute Trump

  2. Paid him generously from public funds

  3. Benefited financially when he spent his earnings on their relationship

Willis initially tried to deny the relationship timeline, claiming it began “after” Wade’s appointment. But evidence suggested otherwise.

Defense attorneys demanded her immediate removal. They argued Willis couldn’t be trusted to prosecute fairly when she had a financial and romantic stake in keeping Wade employed.

Judge McAfee scheduled an extraordinary evidentiary hearing for February 2024. Willis would have to take the witness stand and answer questions about her personal life under oath.

The February 2024 Hearing: Intimate Details on the Stand

On February 15, 2024, Fani Willis did something almost unprecedented: she testified in her own case.

The hearing was extraordinary. For hours, Willis and Wade were grilled about intimate details:

The Timeline:

  • When did the relationship begin?

  • Wade testified: April 2022 (after his appointment)

  • Defense attorneys presented evidence suggesting earlier

  • Cell phone records showed Wade visited Willis’s neighborhood frequently in 2021

The Finances:

  • Who paid for the vacations?

  • Willis claimed she paid Wade back in cash

  • No receipts, no records

  • Wade billed the county $250/hour while taking lavish trips with Willis

The Appearances:

  • Did they cohabitate?

  • How often did Wade stay at Willis’s residence?

  • What did other prosecutors know?

Willis was combative on the stand. She defended her hiring of Wade, insisted there was no conflict, and characterized questions about her personal life as inappropriate.

But her testimony raised more questions than it answered. How could she prove she paid Wade back in cash? Why hire someone with limited RICO experience for such a high-profile case? Why not disclose the relationship from the start?

The testimony was dramatic, personal, and—most importantly—devastating to Willis’s credibility. Not necessarily because it proved corruption, but because it demonstrated staggeringly poor judgment.

Judge McAfee’s Ruling: “Tremendous Lapse in Judgment”

At just 34 years old, Judge Scott McAfee found himself presiding over the Georgia trial of a former US president

On March 15, 2024, Judge McAfee issued his decision.

He found:

  • An “appearance of impropriety” existed in Willis’s relationship with Wade

  • Willis exercised a “tremendous lapse in judgment”

  • However, insufficient evidence existed to prove an actual disqualifying financial conflict

  • Willis could remain on the case IF Nathan Wade resigned immediately

Within hours of the ruling, Nathan Wade resigned.

Willis survived—barely. She could continue prosecuting Trump. But her credibility was shattered. The case that should have focused on Trump’s criminal conduct became a referendum on Willis’s romantic choices.

Trump immediately seized the moment, calling Willis “corrupt” and the prosecution a “witch hunt.” His attorneys appealed McAfee’s decision, arguing Willis should be completely disqualified.

More importantly: the case ground to a halt. While appeals proceeded through Georgia courts, all proceedings in the criminal case were stayed. No trial. No plea negotiations. No progress.

The delay was exactly what Trump needed.

The Pattern Emerges: Delay Equals Victory

The Georgia case’s trajectory followed a pattern we’ve seen throughout Trump’s legal history:

Step 1: Strong evidence of criminal conduct (the Raffensperger call)

Step 2: Competent prosecution begins (Willis’s indictment)

Step 3: Defense finds technical/procedural issue (the Willis-Wade relationship)

Step 4: Extensive litigation over the procedural issue (months of hearings and appeals)

Step 5: Case stalls while procedural issue is resolved (all proceedings stayed)

Step 6: Time passes, circumstances change (Trump wins presidency)

Step 7: Prosecution becomes impossible or impractical (can’t prosecute sitting president)

Step 8: Case dismissed, Trump claims vindication

This pattern has repeated across decades: housing discrimination, Atlantic City bankruptcies, Trump University, Trump Foundation, the classified documents case, January 6th, and now Georgia.

Trump doesn’t need to win on the merits. He just needs to delay long enough that circumstances make prosecution impractical.

In Georgia, Willis’s relationship scandal provided the delay mechanism. By the time the appellate courts resolved whether she could continue prosecuting, Trump had won the presidency.

And once Trump became president again, the Georgia case—like all the others—became effectively unprosecutable.
